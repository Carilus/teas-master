Everything you need to Pass the TEAS English test section

The TEAS English test is a section of the TEAS general test that challenges a candidate’s grammar and sentence structure skills. It also tests the candidate’s ability to make predictions based on provided passages.

This article includes everything you need to know to prepare for the TEAS English section. The provided information includes insights into the many questions, topics covered, and ways to study effectively.

About the TEAS English test

  • ● Number of questions: 37
  • ● Duration: 37 minutes

The TEAS English test section has 33 scored questions and four unscored questions totaling 37. We advise candidates to spend about 37 seconds on each question before moving on to the next. This gives them extra time to evaluate their answers at the end of the section or test. Additionally, we advise candidates to do their best on each question since they won’t know which questions are scored and which are not.

Frequently tested concepts

Frequently tested concepts on the TEAS English test include:

  1. ● Conventions of Standard English: The tested concept has 12 questions and involves testing the use of patterns of standard English pronunciation, correct sentence structure, and practices of standard English spelling.
  2. ● Knowledge of language: The tested concept has 11 questions and involves using grammar to improve clarity in writing, developing a well-organized paragraph, and evaluating whether a candidate’s language use meets a particular audience’s needs.
  3. ● Using vocabulary and language to express ideas in writing: The tested concept has ten questions and involves testing the candidate’s ability to determine word meanings by analyzing parts of a word and applying basic knowledge from the writing process for effective communication.

Candidates should study various English topics, including:

  1. ● Determining the meaning of words and their context when used in a sentence.
  2. ● Interpreting subject-verb agreement rules.
  3. ● Accurate use of verb tenses
  4. ● Identifying and using various parts of speech. These include pronouns, possessives, adverbs, verbs, and adjectives.
  5. ● Identifying correct name, address, and title capitalizations.
  6. ● Determining correct spellings for hyphenated (or compound) words, irregular plurals, and frequently misspelled words.
  7. ● Identifying proper sentence punctuation.
  8. ● Change from passive voice to active voice.
  9. ● Differentiating between complex, compound, and simple sentences.

What to study for your TEAS English test

Nouns and pronouns

Nouns are words that name a thing, place, or person, i.e., son, chalk, or school. You can break down nouns into various categories depending on what they do. For instance, common nouns refer to general things like the Eiffel tower or John. The sentence, “The eggs were scrambled in the hot pan.” has two common nouns: eggs and pan because they are objects in the sentence. Proper nouns are things, places, or people with specific titles or names. For instance, dad is a common noun, but their name (or proper noun) is Drew. You should always capitalize proper nouns in any sentence. Collective nouns refer to multiple numbers of objects or a collective group. For instance, class is a collective noun in the sentence: A class of students.

Pronouns replace nouns to prevent sounding repetitive in a sentence or paragraph. They come in singular, plural, and possessive forms. They also come in personal, nominative, and objective forms. Pronouns help eliminate redundancies in a sentence or paragraph, making it easier for people to read and communicate effectively.

Adjectives are words that describe a noun and are used to shed light on what the speaker wants to say about the object. For instance, in ‘She is a smart student.’, the word ‘smart’ describes the object ‘student.’

Adverbs help portray events more clearly and make sentences more colorful. You can find an adverb in a sentence by asking when, where, how, and to what extent. Adverbs are modified verbs and are used to describe comparisons between two things.

Prepositions help specify how something relates to something else in space and time. They connect pronouns, nouns, other words, and elements. For instance, in the sentence, “The dog swam in a lake, " the worn ‘in’ is a preposition specifying where the swimming occurs.

The subject is an idea, thing, place, or person that is doing or being. Sometimes the subject can be called the naming part of a sentence or clause, showing what the sentence is about. It also shows what or who is acting in a sentence. It is most often a noun-phrase, pronoun, or noun.

The sentence structure section covers questions on various types of sentences to ensure candidates write interesting and engaging text. Also, proper sentence structure helps make it easier for candidates to convey their ideas and thoughts.

Fragments are incomplete sentences that do not have a subject, verb, or both. Run-on sentences are two joined independent clauses with no intervening punctuation. This set of questions helps determine whether the candidate can make good sentences that readers can understand.

This set of questions helps determine the candidate’s ability to write well-formed paragraphs, have a good structural pattern, and maximize coherence. Candidates should learn how to create a good paragraph with a topic sentence, some supporting sentences, and a concluding sentence.

This set of questions is meant to gauge the candidate’s ability to understand what a word means based on the context of its use. The candidate is supposed to look at other words within the text to determine the context of the word being tested. Candidates can also determine a word’s meaning based on the context it’s used in.
